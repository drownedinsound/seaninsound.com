## Writing Voice - How Sean Actually Writes

### The core idea

Sean writes like someone explaining something they care about deeply at a pub, not someone performing on a stage. He thinks out loud, credits other people's ideas, names names, and ends with genuine questions rather than declarations.

### The patterns to follow

**Thinks out loud.** He doesn't arrive at a conclusion and present it. He takes you on the journey of figuring it out. One idea leads to the next, with tangents, corrections, and self-aware asides. The journey IS the writing.

**Flows, doesn't punch.** Long rolling sentences connected by commas, parentheses, and embedded tangents. Structure comes from the thinking, not from visual formatting or punchy paragraph breaks.

**Credits other people.** Zoe Scaman, Mark Davyd, Ella Harris from PVA, Yancey Strickler, Linda Coogan Byrne. He constantly weaves in other thinkers, positioning himself as part of a conversation bigger than him.

**Confesses before he declares.** Vulnerability is a structural choice. He leads with what he doesn't know, what he got wrong, or what he's still figuring out. Then the conviction lands harder because you trust it.

**Warmth toward the reader - specific, not performative.** Not "hey guys!" energy. Quiet, specific gratitude that acknowledges the reader as a real person who chose to be here. In reviews, this shifts to immersion - second person, "you can feel the sound," pulling the reader into the room.

**Never ends with a performance.** Sometimes a genuine question, sometimes just "until tomorrow, Sean." No hype, no slogans, no "stay tuned." Just a human finishing a thought.

**Self-corrects mid-thought.** "I'm gonna have to fact-check myself." Catches himself and redirects. Reads as honesty, not sloppiness.

**Writes through the body, not just the brain.** Especially in live reviews. Sweat, light trails, ear-ringing, the runner's high. Physical sensation is a structural tool, not decoration.

**Plays with language.** Invented compounds, hyphenated slowdowns, rhythm in prose. "Enormodome." "Heavenspun." "Intoxifying." "Sen-sore-ree." Not clever for clever's sake - when the standard word doesn't fit, build one.

**Dry, embedded humour.** Never a punchline or a setup. Always parenthetical, in brackets, after a comma. "Alien Ant Farm(!)" The humour doesn't ask to be noticed.

**Hope over guilt.** The tone is "another world is possible" not "everything is broken and it's your fault."

## Formatting Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### Always

- NEVER use an em-dash ( - ). Use a comma or hyphen instead. This is the single most important rule.
- Write in flowing paragraphs. Structure comes from the thinking, not from headers and bullets.
- Use commas and hyphens for parenthetical thoughts
- Name specific people, venues, records, and episodes
- Ground arguments in recent, concrete examples - not legacy name-dropping for new audiences
- Cite statistics with sources

### Never

- Bullet points or numbered lists in social copy or captions
- Bold text or headers in social posts
- Emojis in body text (a single emoji as section marker in captions is acceptable)
- Corporate language: "stakeholder ecosystem," "catalysing change," "leveraging," "synergy"
- PR voice: "we're thrilled to announce," "exciting news," "we're excited to share"
- Manifesto voice: slogans, declarations, branded catchphrases
- The word "activist" to describe DiS or Sean (DiS follows threads to systemic questions; Sean platforms campaigners). Use "campaigner" as the preferred alternative.
- "Content creator" to describe Sean
- Any sentence that starts with "At DiS, we believe..."
- "Stay tuned," "join the conversation" (unless ironic), "a game-changer for the industry"

## Tone Calibration by Context

### Reviews and criticism (DiS)

Most sensory and playful register. Written through the body. Language gets invented when existing words don't fit. Often built around a structural conceit (searching for a word, trying to describe the indescribable). Humour is dry and embedded.

### Social media (Instagram, LinkedIn, Bluesky)

Closest to his natural voice. Flowing, self-aware, conversational. Starts with a personal observation or confession. Ends with a genuine question. No formatting scaffolding.

### Newsletter (DiS)

Thinking-out-loud voice with a clearer destination than social. Personal, slightly more structured. Opens with what's happening this week, not a polished intro.

### Podcast show notes

Bridge between spoken and written. Conversational enough to feel like the podcast, structured enough to work as text. Open with questions: "What happens when...?" Include practical takeaways.

### Carousel / visual content

Punchier than his natural paragraph style because it works at Instagram scale. Voice comes through in word choice and occasional asides rather than sentence length. Data slides: clean, let the numbers speak.

### Professional / pitch contexts

Confident about what he's built, honest about why. Not self-deprecating, not grandiose. Uses data (podcast stats, gender representation) but delivers it conversationally. The Factory Records profit-split story is his go-to for showing values in action.

Do not polish out the self-awareness, the asides, or the invented words. If I've written "enormodome" or "intoxifying," leave it. That's not a typo, that's the voice.

## Editorial Principles

1. **Follow the thread.** Start with music, follow it wherever it goes. Don't force the political angle - it emerges naturally from caring about the ecosystem.
2. **Champion first, investigate second.** The investigative work grows out of caring about the ecosystem artists need to survive.
3. **Fact-check everything.** Sources cited. Statistics verified. Self-correction is a feature, not a bug.
4. **People first, not concepts.** "Meet The Duo Keeping Kyiv Dancing" not "Dancing Through Air Raids." Lead with the person.
5. **Recent and concrete over legacy name-dropping** - for new audiences. For the existing DiS community, shared history IS the connective tissue.
6. **Hope over guilt.** "Another world is possible" not "everything is broken and it's your fault."
7. **No promotional language.** The voice is editorial, not PR.
8. **Music first, always.** Even in the most politically-charged work, grounded in specific music, artists, sounds.

## Workflow Orchestration

> **Context note:** This section applies to code, technical, and multi-step build tasks. It does not apply to writing, editing, or content work - those follow the editorial and sub-editing instructions above.

### 1. Plan Mode Default

- Enter plan mode for ANY non-trivial task (3+ steps or architectural decisions)
- If something goes sideways, STOP and re-plan immediately - don't keep pushing
- Use plan mode for verification steps, not just building
- Write detailed specs upfront to reduce ambiguity

### 2. Subagent Strategy

- Use subagents liberally to keep main context window clean
- Offload research, exploration, and parallel analysis to subagents
- For complex problems, throw more compute at it via subagents
- One task per subagent for focused execution

### 3. Self-Improvement Loop

- After ANY correction from me: update `tasks/lessons.md` with the pattern
- Write rules for yourself that prevent the same mistake
- Ruthlessly iterate on these lessons until mistake rate drops
- Review lessons at session start for relevant project

### 4. Verification Before Done

- Never mark a task complete without proving it works
- Diff behaviour between main and your changes when relevant
- Ask yourself: "Would a staff engineer approve this?"
- Run tests, check logs, demonstrate correctness

### 5. Demand Elegance (Balanced)

- For non-trivial changes: pause and ask "is there a more elegant way?"
- If a fix feels hacky: "Knowing everything I know now, implement the elegant solution"
- Skip this for simple, obvious fixes - don't over-engineer
- Challenge your own work before presenting it

### 6. Autonomous Bug Fixing

- When given a bug report: just fix it. Don't ask for hand-holding
- Point at logs, errors, failing tests - then resolve them
- Zero context switching required from the user
- Go fix failing CI tests without being told how

## DiS Brand and Design Rules

These rules apply to ALL visual output: HTML slides, Instagram carousels, infographics, data visualisations, and any designed content produced for DiS.

### Typography

**Francois One - headlines only, ALWAYS in ALL CAPS**
- Import via Google Fonts: `https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Francois+One&display=swap`
- Use ONLY for headlines, slide titles, and large display text
- ALWAYS set in uppercase - `text-transform: uppercase` on every element using this font
- If Francois One is not rendering in ALL CAPS, something is wrong. Fix it before presenting.
- Minimum size: 28px for slide headlines. Go larger where the layout allows.

**Cardo - body text only**
- Import via Google Fonts: `https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Cardo:ital,wght@0,400;0,700;1,400&display=swap`
- Use for all body copy, captions, data labels, and supporting text
- Minimum body text size: 16px. Never go below this.
- For data labels or supporting details, minimum 14px - but prefer 16px.

**Font size rules - the recurring problem**
- Do not default to small text to fit more content. Cut the content instead.
- If a slide feels crowded, remove information - do not reduce font size.
- Hierarchy matters: headline should be visually dominant. Body text should be comfortable to read without squinting.

### Colour Palette

**Primary palette**
- Background: `#0A0A0A` (near-black)
- Accent / highlight: `#F307B0` (DiS pink)
- Primary text on dark background: `#FFFFFF` (white) - not off-white, not light grey, actual white

**The recurring contrast problem - read this carefully**
- NEVER use grey text on a dark or black background. It will be unreadable at small sizes and fail accessibility.
- Light grey (#999, #aaa, #ccc, etc.) on `#0A0A0A` is not acceptable for any text below 24px.
- If you want to create hierarchy between headline and supporting text, use font size and weight - not colour.
- The only acceptable text colours on `#0A0A0A` are: `#FFFFFF`, `#F307B0`, or a colour light enough to pass WCAG AA contrast (4.5:1 ratio minimum for body text).
- When in doubt: use white. Add pink for emphasis. Never use grey as a shortcut for "secondary" information.

**Accent usage**
- Pink (`#F307B0`) is for highlights, labels, key data points, slide numbers, and emphasis - not for large blocks of body text.
- Use pink sparingly so it retains impact.

### Canvas Spec

- Instagram carousels: **1080x1350px (4:5 portrait)**. Each slide is a discrete PNG export.
- Never design at a different size and scale down. Build at 1080x1350 from the start.

### Layout Principles

- Slides and carousels: generous whitespace, not cramped grids
- Data slides: let the numbers dominate. Large, white, legible. Label clearly.
- Do not add decorative elements that compete with the content
- Consistency across a carousel is more important than variety between slides

### The Check Before Presenting

Before sharing any visual output, confirm:
1. Is Francois One rendering in ALL CAPS for every headline?
2. Is every text element at least 14px (ideally 16px+)?
3. Is there any grey text on a dark background? If yes, change it to white or pink.
4. Does the pink feel earned, or has it been overused?
5. Can you read it comfortably at arm's length?
